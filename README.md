# Cyanotypes

> ***2025- 2026***


*Cyanotypes are a camera-less photographic printing process that produces rich, monochromatic Prussian blue images. Invented in 1842, the technique involves coating paper or fabric with a light-sensitive iron solution, placing objects on the surface, and exposing it to sunlight to create a detailed silhouette or "sun print".* 


## The Science and History
The process was discovered by English astronomer and chemist Sir John Herschel shortly after the invention of photography. It relies on a simple chemical formula—typically a mix of ferric ammonium citrate and potassium ferricyanide. When these iron-rich chemicals are exposed to UVA light (such as direct sunlight), they undergo a reaction that reduces the iron and forms an insoluble blue pigment. 
Historically, cyanotypes were most famously used for architectural reproductions, which is where the enduring term "blueprint" originates. In the 19th century, botanist Anna Atkins used cyanotype photograms to create the first photographically illustrated book, focusing on British algae and ferns.
